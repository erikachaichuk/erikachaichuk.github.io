Real estate market analysis

The real estate market is a complex and dynamic entity of a huge interest for investors and policymakers. Analyzing the real estate market is essential due to several reasons.


Firstly, it promotes informed decision making. Such analysis provides with profitable investment opportunities and evaluates potential returns on properties for investors, as well as helps buyers to understand property prices, trends, and affordability before purchasing housing.


Secondly, tracking trends on real estate market gives insights into whether the market is appreciating or depreciating, while analyzing how many properties are available versus sold on the market aids with identifying market imbalances.


The aim of this project is to conduct an analysis of the real estate market by leveraging data visualization techniques using Python programming language.


Specifically, this project focuses on:
1. Deal Satisfaction: comparison of deal satisfaction by country on a scale from 0 to 5, with 0 strongly dissatisfied, while 5 – strongly satisfied.


2. Demographic Insights: examination of age distribution within the market to understand the demographic composition of clients and its implications for the market targeting and segmentation.


3. US Clientele Segmentation: analysis of the geographical distribution of real estate clients in US, segmented by state, to identify state-level trends and opportunities.


4. Revenue Trends: assessment of the total revenues of the real estate market from 2004 to 2010 inclusive to understand the overall market growth.


5. Sale Analysis by Building Type: track and visualization of the total number of sales per year by building type to reveal the demand patterns and preferences in property types.

This project utilizes data from Kaggle. The corresponding csv files can be found on Github.


First, the properties data set was cleaned. The ID column was converted to string type to avoid unintentional numeric operations on the ID series. The column was also renamed to discard the irrelevant metadata.


Second, the customers data set was cleaned. There was an encoding issue with the ID column in the “properties”, and the first column in the “customers_raw_data” was also compromised. The column was thus renamed. Next, the entity column was turned from categoric to numeric by being renamed to ”individual” with values being mapped to 0 and 1. The “mortgage” variable was mapped to 0 and 1 as well.


Third, the two datasets were combined. That was possible through the “customer_ID” being the same feature in both tables. The initial merge resulted in missing some customer data because IDs in the “properties” table were flanked with empty spaces.


To overcome this problem, .strip() method was used, which removed the unnecessary spaces in both tables “customer_ID” series. The second attempt was successful, hence the joint table named “real estate data” was created. Before processing further, the data types of the combined table were verified.


The analysis phase began with calculating the age at purchase by taking it from the “birth_date” and “date_of_sale” fields.


Descriptive statistics and interval-based age groupings provided insights into the customers demographics. Property prices were similarly analyzed by grouping them into price intervals, getting the distribution of the sold and unsold properties.


To examine therelationship between age and property prices, covariance and correlation was utilized. The covariance revealed the extent of the relationship and whether the age impacts the price of properties. The correlation coefficient helped to explore the relationship further.


Geographical and categorical insights were explored through breakdowns. For example, the properties were analyzed by building to see the trends in average price, area, and deal satisfaction. A state-level breakdown included frequency, relative frequency, and cumulative frequency calculations.

The first bar chart represents the age distribution of the property purchases. The age at which most customers purchase housing is in the
"40 to 50" years old bracket. This aligns with the typical age range when individuals achieve financial stability, allowing for
significant investments like property purhcases. The primary contributors to the market are thus middle-aged individuals.
This distribution emphasizes the importance of targeting middle-aged buyers in marketing strategies for the real estate market.


The second bar chart illustrates deal satisfaction by country. The highest satisfaction levels are observed in Canada and Germany.
This may reflect efficient property markets in these countries. Denmark's low score might highlight the need for an intervention.


This graph represents the US clients segmentation by state. It is vidibke that California leads significantly, far surpassing other states.
This suggests that California is the largest market for apartments among the states. The cumulative frequency curve shows that
a few states, California and Nevada, account for a large proportion of total sales. The curve flattens as it is approaching 100%,
indicating diminishing contributions from smaller markets. Overall, the data reveals a highly concentrated market, with California
and Nevada making up the majority of sales. This concentration likely reflects population density, demand, or other economic factors.
At the same time, states with lower sales, such as Utah and Kansas, may represent untapped markets with growth potential.


The fourth graph represents the total revenue on the global real estate market. The sharp rise and fall might indicate reliance
on market trends or external factors, suggesting volatility in revenue generation. It was observed that 2007 was by far
the most popular year for sales. Taking into consideration the historical events, it is logical as it happened right before
the market crash in the US in 2008. In fact, there are also no proper sales in 2009. The post-2007 decline shows the importance
of adapting strategies which would mitigate the effects of external shocks.


The fith graph represents the total sales, segmented by building type. The sharp peak in 2007, followed by a steep decline,
shows again the market's volatility. This data could imform strategies to prepare for market downturns and identify periods
of growth for future planning.


Based on the data, it can be concluded that most sold building were types 2 and 3. However when examining the average price
and deal satisfaction, it is noticeable that type 4 has the highest average sale price at 290.000USD. The average deal satisfaction
is also the highest for the type 4. Type 4 is also the highest in terms of area. It can be concluded that the type 4 buildings are
more luxurious or spacious. This type of property is also not sold as often as others. Customers are more likely to buy smaller
and less expensive buildings, as indicated by the trend observed in the dataset. Real estate developers can consider this
information when devising their strategies. One option is to rely on more standard building types, such as 2 and 3,
and sell more buildings at more average price. Another option is to invest in a few more luxurious properties
and sell them at a higher price, thus creating a higher deal satisfaction.


The last graph represents the relationship between median housing price and population in California. It is visible from the graph
that population density is correlated with higher housing prices, particularly in coastal and metropolitan areas. This visualisation
highlights the disparity in housing affordability between urban and rural areas in California,
which is essential for policy-making and investment decisions.


Overall, it has been established that market trends are highly sensitive to economic cycles, with 2007 serving as a peak year before a sharp downturn due to external factors. It has also been seen that middle-aged buyers dominate the market, indicating a need to tailor services and strategies on the real estate market to this financially active group. Moreover, markets with lower sales and satisfaction levels, such as Denmark or less populated U.S. states, offer untapped potential for development and customer-focused improvements. Lastly, as have been seen in California, which is the region with the highest number of apartments sold per year, the urban regions may remain the epicenter of activity in some cases. This might come as a result of high population density and demand.
